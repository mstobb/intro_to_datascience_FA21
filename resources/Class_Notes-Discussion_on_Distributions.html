Discussion on Distributions¶

  • We need to recall some information from our previous readings:

    • Random Variables are mappings (or assignments) from real random events to numbers
    • RVs give us a way to discuss the frequency and variation of events
      • We can compute centrality and dispersion measures for random variables
        • Mean, median, mode
        • Variance, standard deviation

Probability¶

  • The probability of a random event happening is the chance, or likelihood, of that event
  • The probability of a particular random event, $A$, are defined as the following ratio:
$$ \frac{\text{Number of Ways to Obtain Event } A}{\text{Total Number of Possible Events}} $$

For example, if we want to calculate the probability of rolling a 4 on a dice, we count the number of ways to obtain a 4 (there is only one way), and we divide by the total number of possible events (six different events, because it's a six sided dice). Thus the probability of rolling a 4 is $1/6$.

Quick Excercise¶

Let us assume we will roll two regular dice, each with six sides. After rolling the two dice, we add their numbers and the result will be our total. What is the probability of getting a total of 4?

In [ ]:

  • Question 1: How many total possible events are there?

    • 6 possibilities for the first dice
    • 6 possibilities for the second dice
    • So 6*6 = 36 total possible dice rolls

  • Question 2: How many ways could we get a total of 4?

    • 1 + 3 = 4
    • 2 + 2 = 4
    • 3 + 1 = 4
    • Three ways!

  • So the probability of getting a total of 4 is $$ \frac{3}{36} = \frac{1}{12} .$$

We could easily do this for the other possible outcomes of rolling a dice. For example:

  • What is the probability of getting a total of 7?
    • There are six ways to get seven!
      • 1 + 6
      • 2 + 5
      • 3 + 4
      • 4 + 3
      • 5 + 2
      • 6 + 1
    • So the probability of getting a total of 7 is $$ \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6} .$$

Probability Distribution Function¶

  • A mathematical function that gives the probability of all events for a particular random variable
  • Often written as $P(x)$, where $x$ is the random variable

Example:

We usually first need to clearly define our random variable. Let's do that for our example from before:

$$ x = \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if }(1+1) \\\\ 3 & \text{if }(1+2,2+1) \\\\ 4 & \text{if }(1+3,2+2,3+1) \\\\ 5 & \cdots \\\\ 6 & \cdots \\\\ 7 & \cdots \\\\ 8 & \cdots \\\\ 9 & \cdots \\\\ 10 & \cdots \\\\ 11 & \text{if }(5+6,6+5) \\\\ 12 & \text{if }(6+6) \\\\ \end{cases} $$

Now that we have the random variable defined, we can write down the probability distribution function for that random variable as follows:

$$ P(x) = \begin{cases} 1/36 & \text{if }x=2 \\ 2/36 & \text{if }x=3 \\ 3/36 & \text{if }x=4 \\ 4/46 & \text{if }x=5 \\ 5/36 & \text{if }x=6 \\ 6/36 & \text{if }x=7 \\ 5/36 & \text{if }x=8 \\ 4/46 & \text{if }x=9 \\ 3/36 & \text{if }x=10 \\ 2/36 & \text{if }x=11 \\ 1/36 & \text{if }x=12 \end{cases} $$

Properties of Probability Distribution Functions¶

  1. All probabilities MUST be between 0 and 1
    $$ 0 \leq P(x) \leq 1 $$

  2. The sum of all probabilities, over all events, must equal 1
    $$ \sum P(x) = 1 $$

Computing the Mean using a PDF¶

  • We had previously learned that the mean of a data set is defined as the following:
    $$ \bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i $$
  • We can also compute the mean using the Probability Distribution directly: $$ E[X] = \sum_{x\in X} x P(x) $$
  • When we have a large collection of data, these should give the same number.

Continuous Random Variables¶

  • The Random Variables discussed above are all discrete random variables
    • There are a finite number of possible events
  • Continuous random variables have infinitely many possible events
    • Example: Your height, weight, and average driving speed
    • Probabilities for individual and specific values do not make sense
      • What's the probability of being EXACTLY 6ft tall?
    • Instead, we can talk about the probability of being in ranges of possible values
      • What's the probability of being ABOVE 6ft tall?
  • While slightly more complex, these continuous random variables are extremely useful

Theoretical Distributions¶

  • Instead of observing real-world data and finding their distributions, we can instead use mathematical reasoning to create theoretical ones
  • These theoretical distributions allow us to use an idealized function to describe real world data
    • Real world distributions (especially for continuous random variables) are usually very complex and generally unknowable

Continous Distributions¶

  • The Normal Distribution is a very common, continuous distribution. It goes by several other names:
    • Gaussian Distribution
    • Bell Curve
    • Normal Curve
  • While it is a model distribution, but it does a very good job describing many common phenomena
    • Why? Because it can be proven that the sum of many independent processes (each of which can be a random variable) will always approach a Normal distribution
  • The function that defines the shape of the Normal distribution is fairly complex:
$$ N(\mu, \sigma) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} $$

  • The average of a Normal distribution is determined by the $\mu$ parameter and the standard deviation is $\sigma$.
  • This makes it easy to create and work with the Normal

Sampling¶

  • The above distributions, both discrete and continous, theoretical or emprical, exist independent of any actual events taking place
  • Once we observe an actual event happen, we have realized an instance of the random variable
  • A sample is a collection of observations of a particular random variable
    • The sample and the distribution are not the same thing

Central Limit Theorem¶

  • We said earlier that some processes will approach a Normal distribution eventually
  • This can directly be shown by a simple simulation:
  1. Go to each classroom of students and ask each student how many cups of coffee they drink a day
    • Each student will have some number, maybe several are zero
  2. For each classroom, add up the numbers given by the students
  3. Repeat this process, going from room to room.

While the distribution of the number of cups of coffee each student drinks will not be normally distributed (it's not even continuous!) the SUM of it will be
